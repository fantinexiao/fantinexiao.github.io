About Me

CV

Hi! I am a third-year PhD candidate at the Erasmus School of Economics, Erasmus University Rotterdam.

My research focuses on emotion and ambiguity in social interactions. I study how individuals behave when they send or receive emotional communication, and whether their decisions can be explained by ambiguity attitudes and a-neutral beliefs (beliefs that are not biased by ambiguity attitudes).

To study this, I mainly design online experiments that use emotional communication as treatment variations.

I am also interested in topics related to gender, including gender stereotypes, fertility decisions, and childcare.

Work in Progress

Aggressive toward everyone but powerful men? Strategic display of anger expression

Job Market Paper

Abstract

This project investigates the strategic use of anger expression in social interactions and how these strategies depend on a counterpart's gender and power. I propose that people may direct more aggression toward females partly for strategic motivation, expecting female counterparts to yield greater concessions in response to anger, even when they hold high-status positions. In an online experiment, I examine whether participants choose to send angry messages to counterparts of varying gender and power in negotiation tasks, and what factors explain their choice of expression. I compare participants' expected concessions with actual payoff to identify systematic belief biases. I also analyze how gender pairings influence which anger strategies produce higher payoffs.

Anger as a Signal: Decomposing the Affective, Inferential, and Ambiguity Channels in Negotiation

with David Gonzalez-Jimenez and Chen Li

Abstract

Emotional expressions in negotiation, such as anger expressions, can influence strategic behavior, but the underlying mechanisms remain unclear. This paper develops a structural behavioral framework and uses an online experiment (N = 800) with a 2 × 2 between-subjects design varying counterpart anger expression and gender. We find that anger expressions have no significant overall effect on final offers because they reduce altruism while increasing beliefs about the counterpart's reservation price, with these two channels largely offsetting each other. Anger expressions also reduce perceived social ambiguity, especially when expressed by male counterparts, suggesting that male anger expressions are perceived as more diagnostic signals in negotiation.

The Necessity to Reconcile Choice Inconsistencies and the Dangers to Do It Mechanistically

with Chen Li, Julia Rose and Peter P. Wakker

Abstract

Decision theory has rarely been prescriptive because consultants cannot reliably detect and fix axiom violations in complex preferences. Building on Nielsen & Rehbeck (2022), we propose the low-bar (lb) criterion, requiring procedures to beat blind random choice. We prove that many popular reconciliation methods are lb and can worsen decisions, sometimes inducing expected-utility minimization. We analyze four reconciliation procedures, resolve a reconciliation paradox, and show that in many cases overruling preferences is necessary because less invasive methods are lb. In a preregistered replication using corrected procedures, we find that participants often misunderstand axioms under time pressure. NR's high endorsement rates likely reflect demand effects. This paper identifies new hurdles in prescriptive decision theory and provides solutions.
