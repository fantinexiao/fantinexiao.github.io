About Me

CV

Hi! I am a third-year PhD candidate at the Erasmus School of Economics, Erasmus University Rotterdam.

My research focuses on emotion and ambiguity in social interactions. I study how individuals behave when they send or receive emotional communication, and whether their decisions can be explained by ambiguity attitudes and a-neutral beliefs (beliefs that are not biased by ambiguity attitudes).

To study this, I mainly design online experiments that use emotional communication as treatment variations.

I am also interested in topics related to gender, including gender stereotypes, fertility decisions, and childcare.

Work in Progress

Aggressive toward everyone but powerful men? Strategic display of anger expression.

Job Market Paper

Abstract: This project investigates the strategic use of anger expression in social interactions and how these strategies depend on a counterpart's gender and power. I propose that people may direct more aggression toward females partly for strategic motivation, expecting female counterparts to yield greater concessions in response to anger, even when they hold high-status positions. In an online experiment, I examine whether participants choose to send angry messages to counterparts of varying gender and power in negotiation tasks, and what factors explain their choice of expression. I compare participants' expected concessions with actual payoff to identify systematic belief biases. I also analyze how gender pairings influence which anger strategies produce higher payoffs.

Opening the black box of emotion expression: How anger expression shapes beliefs, attitudes, and tastes in negotiation.

with David Gonzalez-Jimenez and Chen Li

Abstract: This study examines how anger expression shapes negotiation decisions by affecting altruism and signaling counterparts' reservation prices, and how it influences perceived ambiguity. We also investigate whether these effects depend on counterpart gender. Participants acted as proposers in ultimatum and dictator games, splitting a $20 endowment with counterparts whose gender (male vs. female) and emotional expression (anger vs. neutral) were manipulated. Beliefs and ambiguity attitudes were elicited using the belief hedge method, and altruism was measured via dictator game allocations. Anger reduced altruism and perceived ambiguity for male counterparts only, while increasing expected reservation prices for both genders in the ultimatum game. These opposing effects canceled out, resulting in no net change in final offers. Beliefs and perceived ambiguity predicted the gap between ultimatum and dictator offers, showing that participants strategically integrated emotional cues.

The Necessity to Reconcile Choice Inconsistencies and the Dangers to Do It Mechanistically.

with Chen Li, Julia Rose and Peter Wakker

Abstract: Decision theory has rarely been prescriptive because consultants cannot reliably detect and fix axiom violations in complex preferences. Building on Nielsen & Rehbeck (2022), we propose the low-bar (lb) criterion, requiring procedures to beat blind random choice. We prove that many popular reconciliation methods are lb and can worsen decisions, sometimes inducing expected-utility minimization. We analyze four reconciliation procedures, resolve a reconciliation paradox, and show that in many cases overruling preferences is necessary because less invasive methods are lb. In a preregistered replication using corrected procedures, we find that participants often misunderstand axioms under time pressure. NR's high endorsement rates likely reflect demand effects. This paper identifies new hurdles in prescriptive decision theory and provides solutions.
